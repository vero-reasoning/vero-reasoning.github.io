Results

We evaluate Vero on 30 benchmarks spanning six task categories. The same open recipe improves four different base models and reaches state-of-the-art overall performance among 8B open-weight VLMs.

Starting from Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct, Vero improves Chart and OCR, STEM, Spatial and Action, and Grounding and Search by substantial margins, and it outperforms Qwen3-VL-8B-Thinking on 20 of 30 benchmarks without relying on proprietary thinking data. Applied on top of Qwen3-VL-8B-Thinking itself, the same recipe improves further to 23 of 30 benchmarks. Starting from MiMo-VL-SFT, Vero also surpasses MiMo-VL-RL, showing that a transparent recipe can match or exceed systems trained with non-public RL pipelines.

These gains are not restricted to a single capability family. The improvements here span chart understanding, spatial reasoning, grounding, counting, and open-ended instruction following.

Method

Vero trains on 600K curated RL samples drawn from 59 datasets organized into six categories: Chart and OCR, STEM, Spatial and Action, Knowledge and Recognition, Grounding, Counting and Visual Search, and Captioning and Instruction Following. These categories are not intended as a cosmetic taxonomy. They correspond to substantially different use cases, visual inputs, reasoning patterns, and answer formats.

The RL objective itself is intentionally simple. Vero uses GSPO-style optimization with task-routed verifiers, so numeric questions, multiple choice questions, grounding boxes, clicks, ordering problems, and open-ended instruction-following outputs.

Data Diversity and Transfer

We show that single-task RL does not generalize reliably across visual capabilities. Training on one category often improves that category while degrading others, especially Grounding and Captioning and Instruction Following. This is consistent with classic multi-task RL results showing that heterogeneous tasks create interference unless the training distribution is carefully balanced (Teh et al., 2017; Hessel et al., 2019). By contrast, the mixed model produces positive gains across categories and avoids the catastrophic spillover seen in narrow-domain RL.

Behavioral Analysis

Accuracy alone does not explain why broad training helps. We run a behavioral analysis to study the generated reasoning traces using a framework of 34 high-level behaviors and a lower-level skill extraction pipeline (Kargupta et al., 2025; Didolkar et al., 2025). The resulting picture is that different task categories do not simply induce more or less reasoning. They induce qualitatively different reasoning styles.

STEM training increases reflective, backtracking-heavy traces. Grounding and search tasks suppress introspective behavior and favor direct perceptual allocation. Chart tasks produce more systematic regional synthesis, while captioning and instruction-following tasks elevate communicative and descriptive behaviors. These differences help explain why single-task training transfers poorly outside its own domain: the model is adapting not only its answers, but also its latent reasoning policy.

To quantify this separation, the report trains a logistic regression probe on 1,500 extracted skills per domain. The probe reaches high overall accuracy at recovering the source domain from the extracted skill lists, which suggests that the domains cultivate partially distinct skill repertoires rather than a generic increase in chain-of-thought length.
